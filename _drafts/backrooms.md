- I feel like the furniture being clipped through walls is overdone in the movie compared to the YouTube version, and it makes the space feel less liminal. There’s also multiple instances of an object from real life being reduplicated, rotating and shrinking, which again feels overdone. The YouTube versions’ interpretation of the backrooms purely in terms of building form I think was more interesting. In particular, no-clipping furniture takes away from the liminal feeling of the backrooms, which is a bit unfortunate since this might be outside audiences’ first experience with liminal horror. The YouTube videos gradually building up the weirdness executed this better.

- I appreciate that most of the focus was on the original backrooms rather than other “levels” (e.g. the pool rooms or the library), which were given shoutouts rather than being large parts of the film.

- I like the reveal of the monsters being the backroom’s recreation of humans. I don’t really like the look of the faces of the disfigured people, though; they look motion blurred. I feel like making it body horror, taking inspiration from conjoined twins and other birth defects, would be in more line with how the backrooms form, but I could see it taking away from the uniqueness of liminal horror.

- I feel like the movie wasn’t complete, or it was rushed.

- I think that the movie is trying to portray ASYNC as a corporation of scientists devoid of empathy, but the way it is portrayed, similar to Kane’s intersession YouTube videos, I’m not really a fan of how they’re portrayed. I feel like it creates too much mystery for little benefit, and the movie would be fine without showing the scientists’ POV. And it makes it hard to tell whether the scene at the end, where the scientist interviews Mary, is a dream or not, because it feels too dream-like; the scientist is too insane, and the conversation isn’t going anywhere. I feel like they could’ve shown the outside of ASYNC as a generic Silicon Valley office building at least, to give it grounding.

- The movie hints at Mary’s backstory with the handprint in the concrete and the distorted living room in the backroom in a way that makes me feel like it’ll do a big reveal, but it doesn’t deliver on this. I was assuming Mary somehow had a connection to the backrooms, but this was never expanded upon.

- There were four backrooms scenes I think (the one in the beginning, then Clark alone, then with the camera, then Mary). All of them ended in chases (though Clark could’ve been chased by nothing; we only saw a shadow). While I and I think many people enjoy chase scenes, I think there was a missed opportunity on leaning on the unique element of liminal horror, that you aren’t sure if you’re alone. I feel like one of the backrooms scenes could’ve built up tension without yet delivering any real horror (perhaps in the first two backrooms scenes).

- Overall, I think that the movie is decent. There’s a start of a storyline and a well thought out expansion of the backrooms, but the storyline is not very clearly delivered nor completed, so not even watching the YouTube videos would’ve helped make the movie make sense. But the chase scenes, which I think is what people watch backrooms videos for, were executed pretty well and in this particular sense should help set this movie apart from other horror films.
